21What is the difference between SLOs and SLIs — and how do you enforce them in a pipeline?Performance▾
3-sentence answer
SLI (Service Level Indicator) is the measurement — the actual metric being tracked, like p95 latency or error rate. SLO (Service Level Objective) is the target — "p95 checkout latency must be below 500ms, 99.5% of the time." In a CI pipeline I enforce SLOs by integrating k6 thresholds that fail the test if the SLO would have been breached during the test run: `thresholds: { 'http_req_duration{name:checkout}': ['p(95)<500'] }` — this is a hard gate, not an advisory metric.

23How do you identify and fix a performance regression in a microservices commerce backend?Performance▾
3-sentence answer
Performance regression diagnosis follows the signal chain: k6 shows which transaction degraded and when, AppDynamics distributed tracing shows which service in the chain is responsible, and service-level profiling shows the specific method or database query causing the slowdown. The most common root causes in commerce backends: N+1 query patterns (a cart with 10 items makes 11 database calls instead of 1), missing database indexes on high-cardinality columns (order lookup by customer ID without an index becomes a full table scan at scale), and synchronous calls to slow external services (payment gateway timing out blocking the order thread). At Care.com I established performance benchmarks and caught production bottlenecks before customer impact using SignalFx and synthetic monitoring — the same diagnosis workflow applies here with AppDynamics.

Quality becomes siloed when QE is only involved at the end — engineers write code, QE finds problems, and the relationship becomes adversarial because QE is the delivery blocker. I break this by making quality a shared engineering concern from the start: pairing with engineers during development, contributing to design reviews, and making the quality metrics visible to the whole engineering team rather than just the QE function. The shift is complete when developers ask "how do we test this?" during design rather than "can you test this?" after code is written — and that shift happens when QE consistently makes developers' lives easier rather than harder. At Care.com I got to a point where developers were requesting QA involvement earlier in the sprint cycle — that's the signal the culture has shifted.
