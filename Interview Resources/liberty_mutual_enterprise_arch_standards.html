The setup approach — what to do in the first 90 days
Enterprise architectural standards fail when they're designed in a boardroom and handed down. They succeed when they're co-created with the teams who must live by them, grounded in real pain points, and supported by tooling that enforces them automatically. Here's how to do it right at Liberty Mutual GRS.
05 Step 1 — Audit before architecting: mapping the current state across hundreds of systems Setup ▾
The Challenge at Liberty
GRS has several hundred systems at different stages of modernisation — some still on legacy mainframe/SOA patterns, some cloud-native, some mid-migration. You cannot impose one architectural standard across all of them simultaneously. You need to know what you're working with first.
  • What tooling is each team using? JMeter? Manual scripts? Nothing?
  • What CI/CD maturity? No pipeline? Basic build? Full DevTestOps?
  • What observability? DataDog? Custom logging? Blind?
  • What governance? PractiTest? TestRail? Spreadsheets?
Your Approach
Build a maturity matrix — score every system across five dimensions: test coverage, automation maturity, CI/CD integration, observability, and documentation. This creates a heat map of where to focus first.
"Before I define a standard I need to understand what I'm standardising from. I'd spend my first 30 days building a maturity matrix across GRS systems — not to judge teams but to identify where the biggest gaps are and where a quick win would build credibility for the broader standards programme."
Care.com: audit-first approach across 20+ markets · M&A integration assessments
06 Step 2 — Define the non-negotiables: what every system must meet regardless of stage Setup ▾
The Challenge at Liberty
With hundreds of systems you cannot enforce the same standard everywhere at the same time — but some things must be non-negotiable from day one because they protect the business. Insurance systems have regulatory, audit, and claims payment obligations. A missing test in a billing system is not a UX issue — it is a compliance risk. You need a tiered standard: mandatory baseline vs aspirational target.
Your Framework
Mandatory baseline (every system):
  • Critical user journeys have automated test coverage
  • All deployments go through a quality gate — no manual bypass
  • Production observability: health endpoint + structured logging + alerting
  • Defect escape rate tracked and reported monthly
Aspirational target (phased adoption): contract testing, autonomous test generation, self-healing, DataDog APM integration.
Care.com: standardised regression criteria · quality gates · SOC 2 compliance governance

08 Step 4 — Govern through the pipeline: standards enforced by tooling, not by trust Setup ▾
Why this matters at Liberty
Liberty Mutual runs 40,000 employees across 28 countries. Standards that rely on human discipline fail under deadline pressure. The only way to govern hundreds of systems consistently is to encode the standards as automated gates in the CI/CD pipeline — a system that cannot be bypassed, regardless of who is asking. This aligns with Liberty's push toward a continuous deployment model that actively reduces technical debt — quality must be embedded in that continuous flow, not bolted on at the end.
Your Approach
Shared GitHub Actions composite workflows — one central definition of the quality gate, imported by every team's pipeline. When the standard changes, it changes once and propagates everywhere. Gate tiers: PR (smoke + SAST, 10 min), Build (regression + contract, 30 min), Release (full + performance + DataDog SLA check). Breaching any gate requires architectural review board approval to override — not a team lead decision.
Care.com: CI/CD quality gates · 90% reliability improvement · Jenkins + GitHub Actions
09 Step 5 — Establish the QE Architecture Council: governance through community not mandate Setup ▾
Why this is critical at Liberty
With a federated architecture model, standards imposed from above create resistance. Standards co-created by the people who use them create ownership. A QE Architecture Council — with representatives from each GRS domain — meets monthly to: review proposed standard changes, share what's working, surface blockers, and collectively raise the quality floor. This aligns with Liberty's VP of GRS Enterprise Architecture focus on outcomes and data-driven decision making that brings strategy and innovation to reality.
Your Approach
Three rules for a council that works: every member brings one win and one unsolved problem each meeting; shared KPI dashboard visible to all members (not just their own data); and any standard change requires council majority approval before enforcement. The peer visibility of shared data — "your escape rate is 15%, theirs is 4%" — drives improvement faster than any mandate.
"Standards that are mandated get worked around. Standards that are co-owned get maintained. The council model gives domain leads genuine voice in what they're held to — and that ownership is what makes compliance sustainable across hundreds of systems."
Care.com: quality council model · global cross-domain governance · community of practice
The real challenges — what will actually be hard
Every candidate will say "establish standards and govern them." Almost none will articulate the specific, genuine challenges of doing this inside a Fortune 100 insurance company mid-modernisation. These cards give you the honest picture — and your answer for each.
10 Challenge 1 — Legacy system incompatibility: standards designed for cloud-native don't work on mainframes Challenge ▾
Why it's hard
Liberty Mutual navigated from SOA and mainframes to cloud-native — this is still in progress. Some GRS systems are still on legacy stacks where Playwright, GitHub Actions, and Kubernetes don't apply. A QE standard that says "all tests must be Playwright" immediately fails on a mainframe batch system or a COBOL policy administration platform. You must design standards that have legacy equivalents — not just cloud-native defaults.
Common mistake: building a beautiful cloud-native QE standard that immediately creates two classes of teams — those who can adopt it and those who feel excluded. That creates resentment and non-compliance.
Your Answer
Design the standard around outcomes, not tools. "Critical user journeys must have automated regression coverage" is a tool-agnostic standard. Playwright for cloud-native, Robot Framework or legacy API adapters for mainframe, UI automation for thick-client. The outcome is the same — the tool is appropriate for the platform.
"I'd design every standard around the outcome it protects, not the specific tool. 'All deployments must pass a quality gate' is a standard. Playwright is one implementation of it. A legacy system might use a different tool — the gate still exists."
Care.com: Playwright + Selenium + Appium simultaneously · multi-platform QE governance
11 Challenge 2 — Organisational resistance: teams protecting autonomy from central QE standards Challenge ▾
Why it's hard
In a federated architecture model, individual domain teams have built their own QE approaches over years. Introducing enterprise standards is often perceived as "central QE telling us how to do our job." This resistance is especially strong in GRS where teams are under delivery pressure from the modernisation programme — they don't have bandwidth to learn new standards on top of migrating hundreds of systems. Liberty's own architecture leadership requires "proven ability to build alignment across diverse stakeholders" and "change leader who operates effectively in ambiguity."
Your Answer
Never impose — always prove. Pick the team with the most pain (highest escape rate, most manual testing, slowest releases) and offer to help them solve it with the new standard. Make the standard look like help, not compliance. Their success story becomes the sales pitch for every other team. At Care.com the $150K vendor consolidation happened through influence across five engineering teams — zero authority over any of them.
"I don't sell standards — I sell solutions to problems teams already have. When a team sees their release cycle drop from 3 days to 4 hours because of the quality gate framework, they become advocates. That's more powerful than any mandate I could issue."
Care.com: $150K savings through influence · cross-functional adoption · community of practice
12 Challenge 3 — Tooling sprawl: dozens of teams using different tools that don't integrate Challenge ▾
Why it's hard
Across hundreds of systems in a company the size of Liberty Mutual GRS, QE tooling is almost certainly fragmented — some teams on JMeter, some on LoadRunner, some on NeoLoad, some with no performance testing at all. Your Answer

Identify which tools provide overlapping value, which are business-critical to specific teams, and which are genuinely replaceable. At Care.com I reduced vendor spend by $150K through exactly this analysis. At Liberty I'd build a tool rationalisation roadmap — aligned to the enterprise standards — that gives teams a clear migration path with timeline, not a cliff-edge switch.
Care.com: $150K tool consolidation · strategic vendor management · tooling standardisation
13 Challenge 4 — Regulatory constraints: insurance compliance limits how fast you can change QE processes Challenge ▾
Why it's hard
Liberty Mutual GRS operates in a heavily regulated insurance environment — state insurance regulators, Lloyd's market standards, SOX-adjacent financial controls for a publicly reported entity, and actuarial data integrity requirements. Any change to QE processes that touches audit trails, change evidence, or test traceability must be approved through compliance channels before it can be enforced. Your Answer

Test cases are traceable to requirements and regulatory obligations. Change evidence is automatically attached to change records. At Care.com I governed GDPR, CCPA, and SOC 2 compliance as part of the QA process design — the same discipline applies here with insurance-specific regulations.
"I treat compliance as architecture — it gets designed in, not bolted on. At Care.com every quality gate produced audit-ready evidence automatically. I'd bring the same approach to Liberty's regulatory requirements — the standard is more credible when it makes compliance easier, not harder."

20 "How would you approach establishing QE architectural standards across hundreds of systems in GRS?" Interview Q ▾
Your Complete Answer
"I'd start with an audit, not a document. Before I define a standard I need to understand what I'm standardising from — so my first 30 days would be building a QE maturity matrix across GRS systems: scoring automation coverage, CI/CD integration, observability, test management tooling, and documentation completeness. That gives me a heat map of where to focus first and where the quick wins are.

From there I'd define two tiers: a mandatory baseline that every system must meet — automated coverage of critical user journeys, quality gate in the pipeline, production observability — and an aspirational target for teams further along the maturity curve, like autonomous test generation and self-healing tests.

Then I'd build the reference architecture as a working template — actual GitHub repos, pipeline workflows, DataDog integration patterns — not a PowerPoint. Teams can clone and adapt it rather than starting from scratch.

Governance happens through a QE Architecture Council with domain representatives who co-own the standards, and through pipeline enforcement — the gate is automated and cannot be bypassed without an architectural review board exception.

And I'd prove it works on one real system within 60 days — present the before/after metrics to leadership and let that success story drive adoption across the rest of GRS. At Care.com I governed quality across 20 countries using exactly this model — audit first, standards second, tooling third, community fourth."
21 "How do you handle teams that resist adopting enterprise QE standards?" Interview Q ▾
Your Answer
"Resistance usually comes from one of three places: the team feels the standard doesn't apply to their context, they don't have capacity to adopt it on top of delivery pressure, or they've seen standards programmes fail before and don't trust this one will be different.

My approach for each: for context mismatch — I make the standard outcome-based, not tool-prescriptive. 'All deployments must pass a quality gate' applies to every system. How they implement that gate depends on their stack. For capacity — I offer to help them adopt, not just tell them they must. I bring the reference architecture and do the first implementation with them. For trust — I show results before asking for commitment. One working example on a real system is worth ten strategy documents.

At Care.com I convinced five engineering teams to consolidate tooling — saving $150K annually — through influence and demonstrated results, with no authority over any of them. Standards adoption is a sales process, not a mandate."
22 "How would you balance enterprise standards with the autonomy of Agile product teams?" Interview Q ▾
Your Answer
"Enterprise standards and Agile autonomy are not opposites — they operate at different levels. Standards define the 'what' — what outcome must be achieved. Agile autonomy defines the 'how' — how each squad achieves it within their delivery model.

A standard that says 'all PRs must pass a quality gate before merging' is compatible with any Agile framework. What the gate contains, how the team writes their tests, which tools they use within approved options — that's team autonomy. The standard sets the floor, not the ceiling.

I also build standards iteratively — they go into the council for review before they're enforced, teams have a comment period, and the first enforcement cycle has a grace period for adoption. That process respects Agile teams' desire to have input on their own constraints.

At Liberty specifically, with the federated architecture model, this balance is already part of the operating model — local execution within central standards. I'd work within that existing structure rather than creating a new governance layer."
